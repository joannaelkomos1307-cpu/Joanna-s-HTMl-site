Plato’s allegory of the cave describes people imprisoned who mistake their shadows for what's real, never truly realizing that the things they view are just projections created by angles and light. In many ways today, modern photography puts us in the same concept. Photopragphs can feel like they reflect the word, yet they are somehow changed by technology, settings, and the human choices made during editing that we now believe are raw and true. Concepts such as ISO and DPI show us that these images, such as Plato's shadows, are only interpretations of what humans believe the truth is, rather than the truth itself. ​ISO in photography controls a camera’s sensitivity and reactivity to light. A low ISO is used to produce a clean and detailed image while a high ISO can produce a grainy picture. This can parallel the cave prisoner’s incompetence and reliance on the poor lighting that shape their perception because when light is scarce, prisoners compensate at a cost. The resulting image becomes noisy and distorted, not because the outside world itself has changed, but because the means of perception have changed. High ISO noise can block better detail which reflects the firelight in Plato’s cave that can blur the perception of the shadows. In both of these incidents, the light being limited changes the bigger picture. ​DPI, aka dots per inch, can affect how much detail the image holds when it’s displayed anywhere. A low DPI can be fine at first, but the closer you get it reveals pixels and loss of clarity. This mirrors the prisoner’s inability to accept the shadows as their true reality. Without this ability to examine a closer low quality. Here, Plato’s point is that understanding things from a different perspective, much like increasing the DPI, is more nuanced. ​Overall, photography raises multiple questions about the true raw and intentions of its creation. Even a “high-qaulity” image can be changed and create a new perspective due to angles, editing, or focus. Plato’s cave isn’t about poor perspective, it’s about deception normalized by comfort. A sharp photograph can still be a “shadow” when it lacks context or is edited to promote a false perspective. This ultimately makes it an illusion. ​In the digital age, saturated with images, Plato’s cave can feel the most relevant today because of the screens, algorithms, and things viewers see on the daily. Only through constructive criticism on photography can viewers step outside this mindset and recognize images can be created to form opinions rather than the truth. ​In the end, Plato’s messages push people to question appearances and find the true meaning behind everything, little or big. Photography, with its many editable options, shows how reality can be altered to anyone’s liking. The clearer the mage is, the more convincing it can become. But viewers must learn to question everything they see in photography and real life. In doing this, photography becomes a weapon against these “shadows” and a reminder that truth lies anywhere.

In “With ‘AI slop’ distorting our reality, the world is sleepwalking into disaster,” Nesrine Malik warns that the resurgence of low-quality, AI-generated content is not just a product of new technology and innovation, it is actually reshaping our understanding of media around us. At its very root, what Malik calls “AI Slop” involves an overwhelming amount of images and information that can range from cartoonish nonsensical content to highly deceptive propaganda visuals. This new range, Malik argues, creates a large gap in what we see and what we can truly trust. To frame this through the lenses of photomontage, collage, and composition, traditional artistic strategies that shift images, can help clarify the culture shift of the past 10 years. Historically, photomontage and collage were very traditional practices used by many people to break through traditional ways of spreading information by using visual elements. These techniques are used to welcome viewers with open arms and often question authority alongside authenticity. Where did this human intention go? Malik states that by contrast, AI composition is driving the rise of ‘AI slop” because it is the exact opposite of traditional digital media. Rather than a thoughtful idea that changes visuals, AI tools like generative images can immediately stitch together elements and mediums from thousands of creations, creating low-quality soul-less content that doesn’t feel authentic. This AI output now takes place in our generation alongside images of political figures, war, and daily news. This blurs the boundary between reality and fabrication. In traditional photomontage, the artist decides how to make these images to convey critique or their commentary. In AI compositing, that intention is basically non-existent. The algorithm isn’t about artistic expression anymore, it’s about clicks, engagement-bait, and the vitality of said content. Malik highlights how platforms like Facebook and X make this situation worse because it’s the perfect hub for attention and revenue. This transforms the “collage” idea of our feeds and turns it into a chaotic mess where truth and fiction are translucent. More importantly, this resurgence of “SLop” destroyed the trust that images have always he;d. Photographs that once had authority and the trust of people, now are all questioned with the idea of AI in the back of everyone’s mind. When AI compositing can produce such hyper-realistic images that are impossible to tell apart, our ability to rely on visual records completely falters. We become skeptical and Malik writes that this consequence is not just confusion but a "Paralysis in perception,” where we cannot decide what to believe. With this information in mind, AI generated images are a degraded form of composition and collage, not creative or critical work but a produced distorted combination that collapses the trust of what humans have been known to be real. Malik’s article prompts an inner reflection on how these rapidly popular practices of digital media can now challenge the ways humans have long used composition and the ways humans have long used imagery to understand, remember, and interpret the world. Without active efforts to find what's real and what's not, we risk the future where we are no longer controlled by AI slop, but fooled by it.
